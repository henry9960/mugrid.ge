Product management is one of those roles that's difficult to understand until you're doing it. No two companies define the role the same way, and the day-to-day can vary wildly depending on the team, the stage of the product, and the problems on the table.

Here are a few things I've learned across my internship experiences that I wish I'd known going in.

## Talk to users before you write anything

The instinct when you join a new team is to dive into the backlog, understand the roadmap, and start contributing. Resist this. The most valuable thing you can do in your first few weeks is talk to users — real ones, not through dashboards or second-hand summaries.

Understanding how people actually use the product (and where it breaks for them) gives you a foundation that no amount of document reading can replicate.

## Prioritisation is a communication tool

Saying no is easy. Explaining *why* something isn't being prioritised right now is harder and more important. A good prioritisation framework isn't just for ranking features — it's for building shared understanding with engineering, design, and stakeholders about what you're optimising for.

## Metrics are a lagging indicator

By the time something shows up in your metrics, the decision that caused it was made weeks or months ago. Don't wait for numbers to tell you something is wrong. Build the instincts to feel it earlier, through user research, qualitative signals, and staying close to the product.

## Done is a spectrum

Shipping something isn't the end — it's the beginning of learning whether your hypothesis was right. The teams that iterate fast and learn fast tend to build better products than the ones that try to get it perfect before launch.
