Working on AI products introduces a different kind of uncertainty to the product process. The output isn't fully deterministic. Users interact with it in ways that are hard to predict. And the line between "bug" and "unexpected behaviour" gets blurry fast.

Here are some of the ways it's reshaping how I think about building.

## The product layer matters more than ever

When the underlying model can change — improving overnight or shifting in subtle ways — the product experience becomes the stable layer that users hold on to. Consistency of UX, clear affordances, and predictable flows matter even more when what's underneath is probabilistic.

## Evaluation is a product problem

In traditional software, QA is relatively mechanical. In AI products, deciding whether something is "good" is often subjective, contextual, and user-dependent. Building frameworks for evaluation — what does a good response look like? — is itself a product challenge that deserves as much attention as feature development.

## Trust is earned incrementally

Users approach AI features with a mix of curiosity and scepticism. The products that build trust are the ones that are honest about limitations, surface uncertainty when it exists, and let users feel in control. Overconfident AI features tend to erode trust quickly when they fail.

## Feedback loops are different

In traditional products, a user clicking the wrong button is a clear signal. In AI, a user rephrasing their query three times before giving up is a more ambiguous one. Learning to read these signals — and building the instrumentation to capture them — is a new muscle.

I'm still early in figuring this out, but it's one of the more interesting problems I've encountered in product work.
